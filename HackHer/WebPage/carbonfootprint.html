Relationship Between Life Expectancy to Carbon Emissions

People living in countries with low carbon emissions can attain a reasonably high life expectancy.

The relationship between environmental degradation and human health has become a major research topic in both social and natural sciences as the figures such as Greenhouse gas emissions have dramatically increased over the last century. That increase coincides with the concern for the Global Warming, which is the increase of the earth's average temperature because of human induced greenhouse gases in the atmosphere. Apart from global warming, other possible effects of increases in the greenhouse gases could include the increase in acid rains and smog-filled cities which all can have harmful effects on human health. However, it can also be argued that as countries emit huge amounts of greenhouse gases because of industrialization and massiveenergy consumption, which in turn brings economic growth, they can spend more in social security and health care which might have desirable effects on human health. Thus, even though greenhouse gases harm biosphere and human health, it can also faster the economic growth, which will then enable societies to spend more on health care. Therefore, the research onanalyzing correlation between Greenhouse gas emissions and human health could provide important insights to policy makers on debates and agendas concerning the mitigation of the effects of greenhouse gases on human health and the effect of industrialization or energy consumption on healthcare expenditure. In the presence of these discussions, this paper will tryto find a relationship between two variables; CO2 Emissions as an indicator of Greenhouse gas emissions and Life Expectancy at birth as an indicator of years that average people can live.

Type of Study

This study interprets data on two variables; life expectancy at birth and CO2 emission levels of different countries, to make coherent sense on finding out possible significant relationship between them. It incorporates classical data analysis methods; simple linear regression and hypothesis testing to interpret those data and come into conclusions regarding relationships between those two variables. This study is concerned with finding any correlation between those two variables and interpretation of any result than can be accomplished by the study.

Data Collection Methods

This study uses secondary data which is obtained from the databank of the World Bank as its main input. Data regarding the life expectancy at birth and CO2 Emissions are retrieved from the website of the World Bank. The data that is used for this study is corss-sectional data, meaning that the data will be analyzed per differences on CO2 levels and life expectancy at birth of different countries at the same point of time, not per differences on different time frames. The research sample consists of 182 different countries each having its own different levels of life expectancy at birth and CO2 levels. The point of time that will be analyzed is 2011.

Limitations of the Study

This study is limited due to lack of any cause-effect analysis between Greenhouse gas emissions and human health. That is, the findings that are driven from the study will not reveal any cause and effect relationship between those two variables, but rather it will reveal whether any kind of correlation, be it positive or negative can eb driven from this data analysis. In addition, this study lacks any other significant variables that could have been argued to have an effect on this phenomenon, such as health care spending, economic growth, greenhouse gases other than CO2. Therefore, the extent of policy application of the findings that will be driven from this analysis will be limited, as it will not be clear whether the relationship between CO2 emission levels and life expectancy at birth can explained by the differences on economic growth or health care system of the country.

Relationship Between Industrial Processes and Activities to Carbon Emissions

Carbon emissions from industrial production refer to the CO2 released from the physical-chemical process of transforming raw materials into industrial products. The fossil fuels used in this transformation stage are considered the carbon emissions from fossil fuel combustion performed by the industrial sectors and are not considered as the industrial process emissions. For example, emissions from the calcination of calcium carbonate are considered industrial process emissions. By contrast, emissions from fossil energy usage during the calcination process are considered energy-related emissions.

Relationship Between Commercial and Residential Causes to Carbon Emissions

The residential and commercial sectors include all homes and commercial businesses. Greenhouse gas emissions from this sector come from direct emissions including fossil fuel combustion for heating and cooking needs, management of waste and wastewater and leaks from refrigerants in homes and businesses as well as indirect emissions that occur offsite but are associated with use of electricity consumed by homes and businesses.
